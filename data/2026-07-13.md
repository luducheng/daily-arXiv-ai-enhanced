### [1] [Observations of the Halo Star HD 177566](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08858)
*William V. Dixon,Pierre Chayer*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: HD 177566 is a hot halo star analyzed via FUV and optical spectra; it is a post-AGB star evolved from the red horizontal branch, with low carbon abundance suggesting no significant third dredge-up, and its FUV flux is underpredicted by the best-fit model by a factor of two.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To characterize the properties and evolutionary status of the hot halo star HD 177566, particularly its chemical composition and evolutionary history, given its anomalous carbon underabundance.

Method: Archival FUV and optical spectra were analyzed to derive Teff, log g, helium abundance, and elemental abundances. Model spectra were compared to observed Balmer lines, He I lines, and broad-band photometry to determine extinction and evolutionary status.

Result: Teff = 33,000 ± 1000 K, log g = 3.79 ± 0.11, He abundance = -0.86 dex. Carbon is underabundant by ~1 dex relative to other halo stars. Extinction E(B-V)=0.095. The best-fit model underpredicts FUV flux by a factor of 2. The star lies on post-AGB tracks from the red horizontal branch with log C/O = -2.18.

Conclusion: HD 177566 is a post-AGB star that did not undergo significant third dredge-up. The FUV flux discrepancy suggests missing opacity or atmospheric effects in models, and improved He I broadening parameters yield better spectral fits.

Abstract: We have analyzed archival FUV and optical spectra of the hot halo star HD 177566. The star has an effective temperature $T_{\rm eff} = 33{,}000 \pm 1000$ K, surface gravity $\log g = 3.79 \pm 0.11$, and helium abundance $\log N({\rm He})/N({\rm H}) = -0.86 \pm 0.05$. Abundances of 13 additional elements are consistent with those of other halo stars, save for carbon, which is underabundant by about 1 dex. The low-order hydrogen Balmer lines are not well reproduced by our models. The diffuse lines of He I are often broader than predicted, but the use of more recent line-broadening parameters significantly improves the fit. Scaling our best-fit model to the star's optical and near-IR magnitudes yields an extinction $E(B-V) = 0.095 \pm 0.005$, consistent with literature values, but the resulting model underpredicts the star's FUV flux by a factor of two. The star's effective temperature and luminosity ($\log L / L_{\odot} = 3.50 \pm 0.08$) place it on the post-AGB evolutionary tracks of a star that evolved from the red horizontal branch. Its low carbon abundance, $\log N({\rm C})/N({\rm O}) = -2.18 \pm 0.21$, indicates that it did not experience significant third dredge-up while ascending the AGB.

### [2] [Developing Machine Learning Models of Subgrid Turbulent Transport for Quiet Sun 3D Radiative Hydrodynamic Simulations](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08969)
*Rimsha Hameed Syeda,Dustin Kempton,Viacheslav Sadykov,Irina Kitiashvili,Rafal Angryk*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: This paper develops a 3D Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a surrogate model to predict Reynolds stress tensor components for subgrid turbulent transport in solar plasma dynamics simulations, achieving ~31% improvement on diagonal and ~8% on off-diagonal components compared to MLP and physics-based models, demonstrating deep learning's potential for subgrid-scale modeling in solar hydrodynamics.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Numerical modeling of solar plasma dynamics is limited by computational grid resolution, requiring accurate estimation of subgrid turbulent processes (momentum transport, energy dissipation) that are critical but difficult to model with traditional approaches.

Method: A 3D CNN was designed to model spatial dependencies in 3D velocity fields, integrating velocity vector components and scalar features (e.g., plasma density). Various activation functions and architectural designs were explored. Logarithmic data transformation was applied to handle skewed stress tensor data. Performance was compared against MLP, Gradient, and Smagorinsky models.

Result: The 3DCNN outperformed MLP and physics-based models, achieving ~31% improvement on diagonal components and ~8% improvement on off-diagonal components of the Reynolds stress tensor. Logarithmic transformation further enhanced performance.

Conclusion: Deep learning, particularly CNNs, is a viable and promising alternative to traditional turbulence models for approximating Reynolds stress tensor components in the upper solar convection zone and lower atmosphere, effectively capturing subgrid-scale processes.

Abstract: Numerical modeling of solar plasma dynamics is affected by the resolution of the computational grid. This often requires the estimation of subgrid processes related to the small-scale flow turbulence, as these processes play a critical role in momentum transport and energy dissipation. In this work, we investigate the use of deep learning techniques as surrogate models for subgrid turbulent transport in realistic hydrodynamic simulations of the quiet Sun. We describe the development of a 3D Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to capture spatial dependencies in 3D velocity fields, leveraging different activation functions, as well as different architectural designs. We specifically focus on the prediction of Reynolds stress tensor components. The resultant model integrates velocity vector components and scalar features, such as plasma density, to enhance prediction accuracy. We compare the 3DCNN model to other types of models, such as a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) and physics-based Gradient and Smagorinsky models, and show that the final model design reconstructs the Reynolds stress tensor components more accurately. Specifically, a 3DCNN model achieves an average improvement of ~31% on diagonal components and ~8% on the off-diagonal components of the stress tensor. Additionally, we show that applying a logarithmic data transformation of the target stress tensor components, to handle heavily skewed data, improves model performance. Results demonstrate the potential of deep learning, particularly CNNs, to approximate Reynolds stress tensor components for the upper solar convection zone and lower atmosphere, making them a viable candidate for modeling subgrid processes and a promising alternative to traditional turbulence models.

### [3] [Structure of the Stellar Neighborhood of CQ Tau in the Presence of a Companion in an Elongated Orbit](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09272)
*Tatiana Demidova*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: CQ Tau likely hosts a companion (M-star or massive planet) based on 10-year brightness periodicity and a 25 AU disk cavity with a dust ring at ~53 AU. Simulations show an M-star in an eccentric orbit can create the cavity but places the ring too close, while a massive planet in an outer orbit better reproduces the observed ring location.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To investigate the indirect evidence of a companion near CQ Tau, inferred from a 10-year brightness period and a large disk cavity (~25 AU) with a surrounding dust ring (~53 AU), by testing possible companion configurations through simulation.

Method: Simulation of dust and gas interaction near a star with CQ Tau parameters, assuming a companion in a 10-year orbit. Two scenarios were tested: an M-class star in a highly elongated orbit and a massive planet in an outer, relatively binary orbit.

Result: An M-star in a highly eccentric orbit can produce a reduced-density cavity of observed size (~25 AU), but the resulting dust ring is closer to the star than observed. A massive planet in an outer binary orbit can form a ring-like dust structure at a distance consistent with the observed 53 AU.

Conclusion: The companion causing CQ Tau's brightness variations and disk cavity is more likely a massive planet in an outer binary orbit rather than an M-class star, as the planet scenario better reproduces the observed dust ring location.

Abstract: There are indirect signs of the presence of a companion near the star CQ\,Tau. A period of 10\;years was found in the brightness changes. The image of the disk shows an extensive cavity with a size of about 25~AU, surrounded by a dust ring with a distribution maximum near 53~AU. A simulation of the interaction of dust and gas in the vicinity of the star with the CQ\,Tau parameters was carried out, assuming the existence of a companion in an orbit with a period of 10\;years. It was shown that an M-class star in a highly elongated orbit is capable of forming a region of reduced density around the center of mass of the system, with a size close to the observed one. However, the dust ring-like structure in this case is located noticeably closer to the star than observed. Evidence has been obtained that a massive planet in an outer, relatively binary orbit, could form a ring-shaped dust structure at a distance similar to that observed.

### [4] [JWST Edge-on Disk Ice (JEDIce): Vibrationally hot, rotationally cold H$_2$ in the outer disk of Oph 163131 non-thermally excited by UV and cosmic rays](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09407)
*Korash Assani,Zhi-Yun Li,Jennifer B. Bergner,David A. Neufeld,Daniel Harsono,Maria N. Drozdovskaya,Marco Padovani,Emmanuel Dartois,Jennifer A. Noble,Nicole Arulanantham,Alice S. Booth,Yao-Lun Yang,Mayank Narang,Will E. Thompson,Elizabeth Yunerman,Karin I. Öberg,Julia C. Santos,Charles Mentzer,Jon P. Ramsey,Lukas Welzel,Klaus M. Pontoppidan,Melissa McClure*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: JWST/NIRSpec IFU observations of the edge-on protoplanetary disk Oph 163131 reveal an unusual "vibrationally hot, rotationally cold" H₂ emission spectrum, interpreted as non-thermal excitation in cold, dense outer-disk gas, with a combined UV and cosmic-ray model implying a high effective cosmic-ray ionization rate (~1-10×10⁻¹⁵ s⁻¹).


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Constraining ionization and excitation processes in protoplanetary disks is essential for understanding chemical structure, disk evolution, and planet formation pathways.

Method: JWST/NIRSpec IFU observations of edge-on disk Oph 163131, analyzing ro-vibrational H₂ emission lines (1–0 O(2) at 2.627 μm, plus v=2 and 3), spatial morphology compared with CO(J=2–1) and near-IR scattered light, and modeling with UV irradiation and cosmic-ray excitation including collisional de-excitation.

Result: A "v-hot, J-cold" H₂ spectrum dominates with the 1–0 O(2) line and suppressed higher-J emission, spatially extended beyond ~200 au with emission above/below a dark midplane lane. Combined UV (χ_UV=100–1000) and cosmic-ray (ζ~1–10×10⁻¹⁵ s⁻¹) excitation with collisional depopulation of high-J levels reproduces observed line ratios and morphology.

Conclusion: Ro-vibrational H₂ emission serves as a novel probe of cosmic-ray ionization in protoplanetary disks, with inferred high ionization rates comparable to those found in starless cores (B68), highlighting its potential for studying ionization processes across different astrophysical environments.

Abstract: Constraining ionization and excitation processes in protoplanetary disks is essential for understanding the chemical structure and evolution of disk material, shaping planet formation pathways. We present JWST/NIRSpec IFU observations of the edge-on disk Oph 163131, which reveal a unusual ro-vibrational H$_2$ spectrum dominated by the 1--0 O(2) line (2.627 $μ$m), with suppressed higher-$J$ emission despite excitation to $v=2$ and $3$. This vibrationally hot, rotationally cold H$_2$ emission is spatially extended, broadly following the molecular disk traced by CO($J{=}2$--1), with emission increasing above and below a thin midplane dark lane and extending radially beyond $\sim$200 au, where near-IR scattered-light emission is no longer dominant. We interpret the observed H$_2$ emission as arising from non-thermal excitation in cold, dense outer-disk gas, where collisions depopulate higher-$J$ rotational levels within each vibrational manifold prior to emission, producing the characteristic ``$v$-hot, $J$-cold" spectrum. We consider both ultraviolet irradiation and cosmic-ray excitation as contributors to the H$_2$ emission and find that their combined action, together with collisional de-excitation of high-$J$ level populations, broadly reproduces the observed line ratios and morphology. Within this framework, we infer a rather high effective cosmic-ray ionization rate of $\sim(1$-$10)\times10^{-15}$ s$^{-1}$ in the presence of a moderate UV field ($χ_{UV}=100-1000$, in Draine units). These results for disks, together with the recent findings by Bialy et al. 2025 for the lower-density starless core B68, highlight the potential of ro-vibrational H$_2$ emission as a novel probe of cosmic-ray ionization.

</details>
