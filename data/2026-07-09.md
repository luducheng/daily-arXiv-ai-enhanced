### [1] [A Momentum-Regulated Model For Star Formation Efficiency in Giant Molecular Clouds](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06727)
*Erik Bertram*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: The paper presents a minimal analytic framework showing that star formation efficiency per free-fall time (ε_ff) in GMCs naturally converges to ~0.01 due to self-regulation between turbulent dissipation and stellar feedback, with weak dependence on global cloud properties.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand why the star formation efficiency per free-fall time in GMCs is consistently observed to cluster around ε_ff ~ 0.01, and to provide a physically transparent explanation for this robustness.

Method: A minimal analytic framework modeling time evolution of turbulent velocity dispersion via momentum balance between stellar feedback and turbulent dissipation, extended to include gravity-driven turbulence, with efficiency expressed as ratio of gravitational to effective feedback velocity scales.

Result: The model shows feedback- and gravity-driven turbulence converge to similar equilibrium states under typical GMC conditions; ε_ff shows weak sensitivity to cloud parameters; predicted scaling relations (Schmidt-like at cloud scale, Kennicutt-like over cloud populations) agree with observations within a factor of a few.

Conclusion: Despite its simplicity, the framework captures the leading-order interplay between turbulence, gravity, and feedback, providing a physically transparent explanation for the origin and robustness of low (~0.01) star formation efficiencies in GMCs.

Abstract: We present a minimal analytic framework to investigate the star formation efficiency per free-fall time, $ε_{\rm ff}$, in giant molecular clouds (GMCs), focusing on the origin of the observed clustering around $ε_{\rm ff} \sim 0.01$. We model the time evolution of the turbulent velocity dispersion through a momentum balance between stellar feedback and turbulent dissipation, and show that this generically leads to a stable low-efficiency equilibrium with only weak dependence on global cloud properties. We extend the framework by including a phenomenological contribution from gravity-driven turbulence and find that both feedback- and gravity-driven motions converge to similar equilibrium states under typical GMC conditions. The efficiency can be expressed as the ratio between a gravitational velocity scale and an effective feedback velocity scale, providing a physically transparent interpretation of self-regulated star formation. The model provides a simple, physically motivated interpretation of observed gas-star formation scaling relations, including a Schmidt-like scaling at cloud scales and a Kennicutt-like scaling when averaged over cloud populations. Comparison with observed GMC properties shows agreement within a factor of a few and highlights the weak sensitivity of $ε_{\rm ff}$ to cloud parameters. Despite its simplicity, the framework captures the leading-order interplay between turbulence, gravity, and feedback, and provides a physically transparent explanation for the origin and robustness of low star formation efficiencies in GMCs.

### [2] [Nitrogen rises to the top: evidence of enhanced mixing in very massive stars](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06828)
*Pablo Marchant,Tomer Shenar*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: Observed nitrogen enrichment in very massive stars (>100 M⊙) cannot be explained by mass loss alone, requiring significantly more efficient mixing beyond convective cores than current models predict. Enhanced core overshooting can reproduce the enrichment but leads to inconsistent evolutionary tracks. This suggests early mixing processes are missing from stellar evolution models, with implications for high-redshift nitrogen enrichment.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To understand the unexpected high fraction of nitrogen-rich very massive stars observed in the Tarantula Nebula (LMC) and the early nitrogen enrichment seen in high-redshift galaxies, which cannot be explained by current stellar evolution models.

Method: Compiled a sample of 122 stars with M ≳ 30 M⊙ in the Tarantula Nebula (LMC); measured nitrogen enrichment factors; tested varying assumptions on binary fraction, star formation history, mass-loss rates, and core overshooting against observations.

Result: Nearly all stars with M ≳ 100 M⊙ show strong nitrogen enrichment (factors ~5-10). This cannot be reproduced by varying binary fraction, star formation history, or mass-loss rates. Enhanced core overshooting (α_ov ≳ 1) can match enrichment but produces quasi-chemically homogeneous evolution inconsistent with the observed HR diagram.

Conclusion: Efficient early mixing processes operating during or shortly after the formation of very massive stars are required but currently missing from stellar evolution models, with implications for the formation, evolution, and final fates of the most massive stars and for explaining rapid nitrogen enrichment in the early Universe.

Abstract: Recent observations of young galaxies in the high-redshift Universe reveal signs of early enrichment of nitrogen. Extremely massive stars ($M \gtrsim 10^{2}-10^3\,M_\odot$) with strong stellar winds have been proposed as a potential driver of this phenomenon. Here, we show that the observed fraction of nitrogen rich stars with masses $\gtrsim 100M_\odot$ cannot be explained solely by mass loss, requiring significantly more efficient mixing beyond their convective cores than accounted in present evolutionary models. We compile a representative sample of 122 stars in the Tarantula Nebula of the Large Magellanic Cloud (LMC) with masses $M \gtrsim 30\,M_\odot$. Nearly all stars with masses $M \gtrsim 100\,M_\odot$ exhibit strong nitrogen enrichment, by factors $\gtrsim 5-10$. We demonstrate that this trend cannot be reproduced by varying assumptions on binary fraction, star formation history, or mass-loss rates within ranges predicted by current empirical and theoretical models. In contrast, enhanced core overshooting of $α_\text{ov}\gtrsim 1$ can account for the observed enrichment, but leads to quasi-chemically homogeneous evolution that is inconsistent with the observed Hertzsprung-Russell diagram. While the origin of this discrepancy remains unclear, our results, in combination with observational and theoretical constraints on mass-loss rates, suggest the presence of efficient early mixing operating during or shortly after the formation of very massive stars. Such mixing models are currently not included in stellar evolution models. These findings have immediate implications for the formation, radial expansion, evolution, and final fates of stars at the upper mass end, and provide a potential pathway to explaining the rapid nitrogen enrichment observed in the high-redshift Universe.

### [3] [Kinetic Processes to Radio Burst: First Observational-driven Study in Coronal Loops](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.06991)
*Mehdi Yousefzadeh,Marian Karlicky,Artem Koval,Alena Zemanova,Yao Chen,Jun Lin*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: This paper presents a multiscale numerical framework (NLFFF + guiding-center + PIC) to model slowly positively drifting bursts (SPDBs) in the solar corona. Energetic electrons injected near the loop top evolve through magnetic mirroring and scattering, exciting beam-Langmuir waves and fundamental plasma emission. The simulations reproduce key SPDB characteristics (~4s duration, frequency drift) and support plasma emission as the generation mechanism, demonstrating a unified model linking coronal magnetic topology, particle transport, and radio emission.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: To link macroscopic coronal structures with kinetic processes responsible for wave generation in coherent solar radio bursts, using SPDBs as observational constraints to understand beam-plasma interactions in coronal loops.

Method: A three-stage numerical framework combining nonlinear force-free field (NLFFF) magnetic extrapolation, guiding-center simulations of energetic electron transport, and fully kinetic particle-in-cell (PIC) modeling. Background plasma density uses a hydrostatic model consistent with active-region conditions.

Result: Evolved beam distributions predominantly excite beam-Langmuir waves and fundamental plasma emission along the loop, with intensity decreasing from loop top to footpoint. Modest initial beam velocity suppresses harmonic emission. Simulations reproduce ~4s duration and frequency drift of SPDBs.

Conclusion: Plasma emission explains SPDB generation mechanisms. The work demonstrates feasibility of a unified model connecting coronal magnetic topology, particle transport, and radio emission.

Abstract: Understanding the origin of coherent solar radio bursts requires linking macroscopic coronal structures with the kinetic processes responsible for wave generation. We investigate emissions from slowly positively drifting bursts (SPDBs), a specific type of solar radio emission. SPDBs provide observational constraints for modeling beam-plasma interactions in coronal loops, serving as a basis for a multiscale description. We employ a three--stage numerical framework that combines nonlinear force--free field (NLFFF) magnetic extrapolation, guiding--center simulations, and fully kinetic particle--in--cell (PIC) modeling. The background plasma density is described using a hydrostatic model consistent with active--region conditions, producing a plasma--frequency gradient comparable to that inferred from the observed SPDBs spectrum. Energetic electrons injected near the loop top evolve through magnetic mirroring, pitch--angle scattering, turbulence development, and partial precipitation. Evolved velocity distribution functions (EVDFs) are sampled after approximately one bounce period and used in PIC simulations to evaluate emission properties. The results show that the evolved beam distribution of energetic electrons predominantly excites beam--Langmuir waves and fundamental plasma emission along the loop, with the emission intensity gradually decreasing from the loop top toward the footpoint. The modest initial beam velocity of energetic electrons explains the inefficient generation of harmonic plasma emission. The temporal evolution of the modeled emission reproduces key SPDB characteristics, including the ~ 4s duration and frequency drift behavior. These results suggest plasma emission explains the mechanisms behind SPDB generation and demonstrate the feasibility of a unified model connecting coronal magnetic topology, particle transport, and radio emission.

### [4] [Double-periodic pulsations simultaneously detected in mid-infrared and hard X-ray emissions during an X1.5 flare](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07442)
*Dong Li,Yuyang Ye,Xianyong Bai,Xu Yang*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: This paper reports the first robust detection of dual-period quasi-periodic pulsations (QPPs) at ~8.5 s and ~4.6 s in mid-infrared (8-10 µm) during an X1.5 white-light flare, simultaneously observed in hard X-rays and microwaves, with evidence linking the periods to sausage mode oscillations and oscillatory magnetic reconnection.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: Flare-associated QPPs are rarely detected in mid-infrared emission, and this study aims to fill that observational gap by exploring dual-period QPPs across mid-infrared, hard X-ray, and microwave wavebands during a solar flare.

Method: The authors analyzed multi-wavelength observations from AIMS (mid-infrared 8-10 µm), Fermi (26-50 keV), HXI (20-50 keV), and WST (white light) during the impulsive phase of an X1.5 flare. Imaging and differential emission measure (DEM) analysis were used to localize sources and diagnose wave modes.

Result: Dual-period QPPs (~8.5 s and ~4.6 s) were simultaneously detected in mid-infrared, HXR, and microwave emissions. Emission sources in mid-infrared, HXR, and white light are spatially coincident, with mid-infrared emission located at loop tops and double footpoints connected by a hot plasma loop. DEM analysis supports fast sausage waves, and WST confirms a white-light flare.

Conclusion: The observed QPPs are attributed to a quasi-periodic regime of magnetic reconnection, with the double periods likely modulated by quasi-harmonics of sausage waves.

Abstract: Quasi-periodic pulsations (QPPs) have been observed in a broad electromagnetic spectrum, encompassing radio, ultraviolet, white light, X-rays, and Gama-rays. Yet, flare-associated QPPs remain rarely detected in mid-infrared emission. Here, we explored dual-period QPPs in the mid-infrared waveband, hard X-rays (HXRs), and microwave emission during an X1.5 flare on 2024 December 30 (SOL2024-12-30T04:01). Flare QPPs with dual quasi-periods at about 8.5 s and 4.6 s were simultaneously detected in the AIMS 8-10 um, Fermi 26-50 keV, and HXI 20-50 keV wavebands during the impulsive phase. Imaging observations show that the flare emission sources in the mid-infrared, HXR, and white-light wavebands are spatially coincident. Mid-infrared emissions are primarily localized at the loop top and double footpoints, which are connected by a hot plasma loop. These observational features support intermittent and rapid energy release via oscillatory magnetic reconnection during the solar flare. Differential emission measure analysis confirms fast sausage waves in flaring loops, while the WST observation indicates a white-light flare. We localized the flare QPPs with double periods in mid-infrared, HXR, and microwave emissions during a white-light flare. The flare-associated QPPs may be attributed to a quasi-periodic regime of magnetic reconnection, with the double periods likely modulated by quasi-harmonics of sausage waves.

### [5] [Ionized gas emission in protoplanetary disks with the SKAO](https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07571)
*Greta Guidi,Christian Rab,Barbara Ercolano,Michael L. Weber,Claudio Codella,Izaskun Jiménez-Serra,Evgenia Koumpia,John D. Ilee,Enrique Macías,Elena Viscardi,Yinhao Wu,Francesca Bacciotti,Asmita Bhandare,Eleonora Bianchi,Tyler Bourke,Luca Cacciapuoti,Antonio Garufi,Geoffroy Lesur,Vincent Piétu,Linda Podio,Giovanni Sabatini,Leonardo Testi,Claudia Toci*

Main category: astro-ph.SR

TL;DR: This paper investigates the capabilities of the upcoming SKA telescope to probe ionized gas (photoevaporative, magneto-thermal, and MHD winds) in protoplanetary disks using state-of-the-art simulations and synthetic observations, and discusses synergies with other facilities to understand disk evolution and planet formation.


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: The ionized gas component in protoplanetary disks (tracing photoevaporation, accretion, disk winds, and jets) remains poorly understood despite progress in dust and molecular gas characterization. Understanding this component is key to studying disk dynamics, evolution, and planet formation.

Method: State-of-the-art simulations of photoevaporative, magneto-thermal, and magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) winds were performed, generating theoretical predictions and synthetic SKA Observatory observations to assess SKA's potential in detecting and characterizing free-free emission and Hydrogen recombination lines.

Result: The study provides theoretical predictions and synthetic observations that assess SKA's capability to detect and characterize ionized gas tracers (free-free emission and Hydrogen recombination lines) in protoplanetary disks within nearby star-forming regions.

Conclusion: SKA will enable detection and characterization of ionized gas in protoplanetary disks, and synergies with complementary facilities will provide a comprehensive, multi-scale view of disk winds, offering critical insights into disk evolution and planet formation.

Abstract: Protoplanetary disks represent a crucial stage in the evolution of Young Stellar Objects towards the formation of fully formed planetary systems. While substantial progress has been made in the last decades in the characterization of the dust and molecular gas in these systems, the ionized component remains poorly understood. Ionized gas traces important processes such as photoevaporation, accretion, disk winds, and jets, and therefore is key to studying disk dynamics, evolution, and ultimately planet formation. In this paper, we investigate the capabilities of the forthcoming SKA telescope to probe this component in protoplanetary disks within nearby star forming regions. We present state-of-the-art simulations of photoevaporative, magneto-thermal, and magnetohydrodynamic winds, and generate theoretical predictions and synthetic SKAO observations to assess its potential in detecting and characterizing free-free emission and Hydrogen recombination lines. Finally, we discuss synergies with complementary facilities and how they will provide a comprehensive, multi-scale view of disk winds and offer critical insights on the mechanisms driving disk evolution and the onset of planet formation.
